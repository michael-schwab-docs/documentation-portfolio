**Managing Inadequately Funded Life Insurance Policies**  
*Guidance Brief*

**Document Type:** Decision-Support Guidance  
**Audience:** Trustees, Investment Operations Analysts, Fiduciary Reviewers  
**Purpose:** Outline practical options for addressing life insurance policies that are projected to lapse prior to maturity or deliver reduced death benefits due to insufficient funding.

---

## Overview

Inadequately funded life insurance policies—those projected to lapse prior to maturity or mature with a significantly reduced death benefit—are common within trust-owned portfolios. In many cases, policies are maintained under original funding assumptions that are no longer sufficient due to changes in interest crediting, cost structures, or inconsistent premium payments.

Clients may prefer to stay the course, either due to inertia or the belief that limited options are available. However, as the projected time to lapse shortens, the need to evaluate and act on available remediation strategies becomes more urgent.

Even when the time horizon is longer, early evaluation is beneficial. Some options may become less viable over time, and exploring alternatives can identify solutions that better align with the client’s objectives.

---

## Core Remediation Options

The following strategies represent common approaches to addressing inadequately funded policies. Not all options will be suitable in every situation, but they provide a structured starting point for evaluating next steps.

### 1. Increase Policy Funding

In many cases, inadequate funding results from premiums that are no longer sufficient to sustain the policy under current assumptions.

Carriers can typically provide updated illustrations showing the premium required to maintain the policy to maturity or another target duration. If the client has both the means and desire to support increased funding—and the death benefit remains necessary—this is often the most direct solution.

---

### 2. Reduce the Death Benefit

Reducing the policy’s death benefit can lower the funding required to sustain the policy.

This may be achieved by:
- lowering the face amount, or  
- changing the death benefit structure (e.g., from increasing to level)

In some cases, the current level of funding may be sufficient to carry a reduced death benefit to maturity.

Constraints may apply depending on:
- prior premium contributions  
- policy structure  
- riders or supplemental coverage  

---

### 3. Combine Funding Adjustments and Benefit Reduction

A combination approach may be appropriate when:
- the required premium increase is not feasible, and  
- a partial reduction in death benefit is acceptable  

This approach is often used when maintaining the full death benefit is impractical, but preserving a portion of coverage remains important while limiting additional out-of-pocket funding.

Carriers can typically illustrate scenarios where a reduced death benefit, combined with a moderate funding increase, allows the policy to meet its target duration.

---

### 4. Replace the Policy

A new policy—either from the same carrier or a different one—may offer:
- more favorable cost structures  
- improved crediting assumptions  
- greater flexibility in design  

If the insured remains insurable and is willing to undergo underwriting, replacement may result in improved long-term performance.

Considerations include:
- sustainability of projected assumptions  
- surrender charge periods on new policies  
- reduced liquidity during early years of the replacement policy  

---

### 5. Surrender the Policy

If the death benefit is no longer aligned with the client’s needs, surrendering the policy for its cash value may be appropriate.

This may be particularly relevant if:
- policy costs are expected to rapidly erode remaining value  
- the policy no longer serves its original planning purpose  

---

### 6. Evaluate a Life Settlement

As an alternative to surrender, a life settlement may provide greater value by selling the policy to a third party.

General considerations include:
- insured age (typically 75+)  
- policy size  
- transferability  
- projected cost of insurance and life expectancy  

A preliminary evaluation can help determine whether this option is viable before allowing the policy to lapse or be surrendered.

---

## Additional Considerations

More complex scenarios may require deeper analysis. For example:
- multiple underperforming policies within a single portfolio  
- competing objectives among beneficiaries or stakeholders  
- uncertainty around timing of the death benefit  

In these cases, comparing multiple strategies side-by-side can help identify the most suitable course of action.

---

## Conclusion

Managing an inadequately funded life insurance policy can present a range of challenges, particularly as the projected time to lapse decreases. However, multiple remediation paths are typically available.

A structured evaluation of funding adjustments, benefit changes, replacement options, and exit strategies can clarify available choices and support informed decision-making aligned with the client’s broader objectives.
